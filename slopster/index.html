Same logic every time: a real style, a locked palette, three weighted facts, a list of clichés ruled out. Written for you, not left to a mood.

1

What's this poster for?

This decides the facts you'll fill in and how the palette names read further down. Worth settling first.

A security callout and an event poster are driven by completely different information. Forcing both into "what, when, where" is why a lot of awareness content ends up reading like an invite rather than an instruction.

2

Pick a real design lineage

Not a mood. An actual visual tradition the model can imitate properly.

A vague word like "cool" or "fun" gets averaged into whatever the model has seen most, which is almost always the same chalk-texture, drop-shadow default. A named lineage gives it a real, coherent tradition to imitate instead of a mood to guess at.

3

Lock the palette

Pick one combination, or set your own. Either way, capped at three.

"Vibrant colours" invites the model to grab from its whole palette at once, which is how you end up with a rainbow gradient. Capping it to one named pair or trio, and giving that pair a name, is what makes it read as chosen rather than default.

4

The facts that must appear

Fill in the three lines for the poster type you picked above.

Every extra line of copy competes for the same visual weight as the ones before it. Three facts is enough for almost anything. A fourth is possible, but it always costs some of the hierarchy the first three had.

Every extra line competes for the same visual weight. Adding this will push something else down the hierarchy.

5

Ban the clichés

Checked items are excluded from the prompt. Untick anything you actually want.

These are the defaults a model reaches for when a prompt doesn't say otherwise: drop shadow, bevel, a stock photo of someone mid-jump. Naming them explicitly as things to avoid is often more effective than any amount of positive description.

6

Format and anything else

ChatGPT and DALLE read best from a written brief. Midjourney and Stable Diffusion read better as a comma-separated list of tags with their own negative-prompt syntax. Same choices, different shape.
